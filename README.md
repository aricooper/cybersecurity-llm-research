\section{Abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) are rapidly transforming the cybersecurity landscape by enabling both unprecedented advances in defensive capabilities and equally powerful avenues for offensive misuse. This paper examines the dual-use nature of LLMs through three interconnected lenses: (1) the technical performance of frontier models in Capture-the-Flag (CTF) environments, (2) the implications of LLM-driven cybersecurity workflows within government agencies such as the Department of Homeland Security (DHS), and (3) a synthesis of emerging governance frameworks that aim to manage the risks of high-capability models. We survey recent research demonstrating LLM proficiency in structured cybersecurity tasks, including knowledge benchmarks (CTF-Know), autonomous exploitation frameworks (CTFAgent), and advanced threat-detection pipelines. We then contextualize these capabilities within real-world governmental AI deployments and analyze associated risks, including data exposure, hallucination, operational misalignment, and adversarial misuse. Finally, we integrate findings from contemporary governance literature to propose a multi-layered strategy for responsibly leveraging LLMs while mitigating dual-use harms. Through this combined technical and policy perspective, the paper outlines how society may balance innovation, security, and ethical stewardship as LLMs become embedded across digital infrastructure.
